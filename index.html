Bandwidth Inflation Attacks in Tor

I am currently working on a research project exploring bandwidth inflation attacks within Tor. Specifically, we are researching how Torâ€™s bandwidth scanner (sbws) is used to verify bandwidth measurements of relays within the Tor network, and how an adversary can manipulate these readings to gain an unfair advantage in circuit construction. To do this, we have constructed a simulated Tor network using Raspberry Pis acting as various nodes.

SimulaTor

While tools such as Chutney and Shadow allow researchers to create virtual Tor networks, there is no resource to help researchers create a simulated Tor network using physical nodes. While researching bandwidth inflation attacks, I have been compiling information on creating a physical private Tor network and plan to release the SimulaTor framework soon. Replicability is extremely important in research, and I hope that the SimulaTor framework will allow other researchers to be able to easily create simulated Tor networks to verify others' findings.

My Projects

Hacked My Professor

In Spring quarter 2023, I was able to successfully plan and execute an attack against my cybersecurity professor where I coerced my professor. For this attack, I created a convincing replica of a student organization's web page which led to a clone of our school's SSO login page to view the full site. Beyond the technical proficiency required for this attack, I also needed to develop a strong understanding of social engineering to convince the professor to follow through with the attack himself. Leveraging my social engineering skills, I was able to engage my professor in a conversation regarding the controversial organization, which led him to check out the replica website I directed him to. Once here, the site seamlessly redirected to my malicious SSO page inorder to view recorded content supposedly hosted on our schools learning management system. My professor quickly entered his school credentials into the malicious site, which I could have saved for future attacks. This project taught me the importance of social engineering as well as technical competence needed for an adversary to carry out a successful attack. You can view the fake SSO page here

Tic-Tac-Toe

In this project, I worked with a team to develop a Java-based multiplayer Tic-Tac-Toe game following the provided Tic-Tac-Toe RFC. Utilizing a server-client architecture, players were able to connect seamlessly from multiple platforms to host and play games and ensure universal compatibility.
